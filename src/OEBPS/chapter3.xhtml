Chapter Three

A Long Walk Through Long Sentences

By the following spring, Tanya had read more first pages than she could count, and had begun — cautiously, then with growing confidence — to read second pages, and third, and once, on a long train journey to Edinburgh, an entire chapter without stopping.

The trick was not to slow down. The trick, she had decided, was to find a typeface that did not require her to slow down. There is a difference between reading carefully and reading defensively, and for thirty years she had been doing the latter without noticing.

On the Importance of Long Lines

A short paragraph, she thought, is a kind of cheat. Anyone can read three words. The real test is the long sentence — the kind that begins with a clear premise, takes a polite detour through a subordinate clause or two, gestures vaguely at an example, returns to the main thread, and finally arrives, breathless and slightly embarrassed, at a verb.

Such sentences, in a hostile typeface, become a slog. The eye loses its place between one comma and the next; the descenders of the line above tangle with the ascenders of the line below; an m in the middle distance dissolves into something that could equally well be an rn, and the reader, having lost the thread, must go back to the beginning of the line and start again, this time with a finger as a guide.

“A book that requires a finger,” Mr. Trevelyan had once said, “is a book that has been set badly. The page should hold its own line. The reader should hold the book.”

In a kindlier typeface, the same sentence behaves itself. The line-heights breathe. The letters keep to their lanes. The reader arrives at the verb on time, and with enough attention left over to notice that the verb was, in fact, worth waiting for.

The Adventures of a Single Sentence

Consider, for instance, the following: On the first warm Saturday in April, when the magnolias along the embankment had finally agreed to bloom, and when the ferries on the river were once again carrying passengers who had remembered, after a long winter indoors, that the world contained both water and weather, Yvette walked from her flat in Walworth to the South Bank, intending to buy a coffee and to read, for the third time that month, the opening paragraph of a novel she had been meaning to finish since November.

That sentence, set in a hostile face, is a labyrinth. Set kindly, it is a walk. The words are the same. The work the eye must do is not.

Closing Note

Tanya finished her coffee. The ferry crossed. The magnolias, in due course, dropped their petals on the embankment, where the wind moved them around for a week before the rain arrived and made the whole question moot.

She went home and read another chapter. The sentences were long. She did not, this time, need a finger.
