Chapter Seven

The Window

It was the last week of October. Yvette had come down from Edinburgh for the launch of Waves on the Water, and had insisted, against several of Tanya’s protestations and one quite firm refusal, on buying the wine. It was a good wine, the label set in a typeface Tanya could not fault, which she took as an excellent omen. They sat by the window of the small flat above the typewriter factory, watching the rain conduct itself on the car park with its usual misplaced enthusiasm.

“You’ve been quiet,” Yvette said.

“I’m always quiet.”

“You’re unusually quiet. There’s a register of quiet you’ve been in all evening that I would describe as pointed.”

Tanya turned her glass on its base. The wine, she noticed, was almost exactly the colour of the streetlamps reflected in it, which she considered a pleasing accident. “I’ve been thinking about leaving,” she said.

“The factory?”

“The factory. The flat. The whole —” she gestured at the window — “arrangement.”

“And go where?”

“Antwerp, possibly. Better light.”

Yvette produced a laugh of the short, involuntary kind that arrives before the brain has had time to decide whether it is appropriate. “You can’t move countries because of the light, Tanya.”

“Why not? People move countries for less.”

“People move countries for love. For work. Occasionally for tax reasons. They do not generally move countries because the street signage is better laid out somewhere else.”

“The kerning is better in Antwerp,” Tanya said. “I’ve been. I have evidence. The municipal wayfinding alone —”

“I believe you. I believe you completely and without reservation. I simply don’t think the municipal wayfinding of Antwerp is sufficient grounds for relocating your entire life.”

“The light is also better,” Tanya said.

“And the light. Yes. You mentioned.”

They sat for a while. Below, a man was engaged in a losing battle with an umbrella. The umbrella appeared to be winning on points.

“I read your book,” Tanya said, after a pause of the length that in her vocabulary meant I have been thinking about how to say this since before you arrived. “On the train to Edinburgh, the last time.”

“And?”

“I didn’t lose my place. Not once. The whole way through.”

Yvette was quiet for a moment. “That’s the typeface you chose, isn’t it. The new one.”

“It is.”

“Is that — I mean, is that a good thing? I genuinely cannot tell, with you, whether ‘didn’t lose my place’ is the highest possible praise or merely baseline adequate.”

Tanya considered the question with the seriousness it deserved. Below, the umbrella had turned itself inside out. The man holding it had stopped fighting and was simply standing in the rain now, looking at it, with the expression of someone who has just arrived at an important conclusion about the nature of effort.

“It’s the best thing I’ve done,” she said. “In thirty years.”

Yvette refilled both glasses with the quiet efficiency of someone who understood that the moment called for it and was not going to make a speech about it. “Then you’re not going anywhere,” she said. “You’re going to stay here and set the next one.”

“Am I.”

“You are.”

Outside, the man had abandoned the umbrella entirely. He was walking to a waiting taxi with his hands in his pockets, at a pace that suggested he had decided, somewhere in the last thirty seconds, that being wet was a condition and not a crisis. The umbrella remained on the pavement. The rain continued. No one came back for it.

And, in the event, she was.
