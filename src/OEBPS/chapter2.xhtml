Chapter Two

The Mirror Letters

Brendan dropped by on a Wednesday, holding a paperback he had bought in a hurry at the airport. He pushed it across the desk. “Read this paragraph,” he said. “Out loud. Don’t think.”

Tanya read. The sentence began with a word she could not, at first, decide upon: was it bog or dog? The b and the d sat side by side on the page like twins arguing over which had been born first. She paused. She tried again. She got it wrong, then right, then wrong.

“That,” Brendan said, “is what my niece does every evening for an hour. She’s nine. She thinks she’s stupid. She isn’t.”

The Usual Suspects

The mirror letters — b, d, p, q — have a long history of misbehaviour. They are the same shape, rotated, and the brain that has spent its first eight years learning that a chair is a chair whether you walk around it or sit on it must, suddenly, learn that a b is not a d, no matter what angle you approach it from.

“A dog is not a bog. A pig is not a qig. The whole business of reading depends on agreeing that orientation matters — and the brain, frankly, would rather it didn’t.”

Then there are the cousins: n and u, m and w, h and y at certain sizes, i and l and 1 in unfortunate fonts. The lowercase a in some sans-serifs becomes a single-storey shape that is, to a tired eye, indistinguishable from an o.

The Heavy Foot

One trick, Tanya knew, was to give each letter a little extra weight at the bottom — a heavier base, a thicker descender — so that the b and the d could no longer be flipped without the result looking obviously upside-down. Letters with a sense of gravity. Letters that knew which way was up.

She thought of the typewriter ribbon, of the way ink had pooled at the foot of every letter on a wet day. The old machines had known. The old machines had given every letter a small, dependable shadow.

The afternoon ran long. Brendan finished his coffee and left the paperback behind. Tanya kept it on the desk, opened to the first page, as a kind of promise.
