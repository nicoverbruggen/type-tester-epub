Chapter Five

Foreign Tongues

In the spring of 2003, Tanya had taken the night ferry to Hoek van Holland, ridden a bicycle for three days through the polders — a journey she had prepared for by reading a guide to Dutch cycling and had survived by ignoring most of it — and arrived, slightly damp and considerably more determined than when she had left, at a café outside Brügge. She had attempted, in the French she had assembled from a phrase book and two semesters of evening class, to order a croque-monsieur. The waiter had replied in better English than she was going to be using for the rest of the trip. The menu, set in a typeface designed by someone who regarded diacritics as optional decoration rather than load-bearing structural elements, had read CROQUE-MONS EUR.

There had been a space where the diaeresis should have been. The u and the e, parted by it, sat on the page like colleagues who had been introduced at a meeting and discovered they had nothing to say to each other. Tanya had stared at it for a moment longer than was strictly necessary, then put it down and looked at the canal instead, where a heron was standing in the shallows with the philosophical composure of something that had long since made its peace with the world and everything in it.

The sandwich, when it arrived, had been excellent. The sun had been making a sincere effort. She had cycled forty kilometres that morning and deserved a croque-monsieur regardless of how it was spelled. She had filed this thought away under Things To Return To, and then, as was her habit with uncomfortable ideas, had forgotten about it for several years.

The diaeresis on her own name — the small dots that no British keyboard could produce without a sustained negotiation with the operating system — had been a lifelong source of administrative grief. Half the world spelt her name Tanya. The other half spelt it Tanja. Her grandmother, born in Salzburg, had used a combining diaeresis above the j that no font in living memory had rendered correctly; every attempt produced something that looked less like a name and more like a word that had been interrupted, mid-thought, by a very small flying creature.

The Accented Alphabet, Visited

A tour of the diacritics that distinguish é from a plain e, ñ from a plain n, and å from an a that has simply given up. A typeface that handles plain ASCII with elegance but trips over its accents is, in the end, a typeface that speaks fluently to half the world and mumbles incoherently at the other half.

The waiter, that long-ago morning, had brought the sandwich without further discussion. It had been, as noted, excellent. The menu was still wrong when she left. She had looked at it once more on the way out, at the space where the diaeresis should have been, and had thought: the sandwich arrived. The heron was still there. The canal was very still. It was possible that some things mattered less than she had believed, and that this was not a defeat but a reclassification.

She had not, at the time, quite known what to do with this thought. It would be many years before she did.
