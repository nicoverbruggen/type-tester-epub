Chapter Four

Numbers, Dates, and Other Numerals

The receipt was a disaster. It claimed Tanya had spent £1,074.30 on lunch, which would have been impressive even by London standards. The real total, she eventually worked out, was £10.74. Somewhere between the till and the printer, a 0 had become an O, a 3 had become an 8, and the decimal point had taken an unscheduled holiday.

Numerals, she had decided long ago, were the most under-considered glyphs in any typeface. Designers spent months on the lowercase g and shipped the digit 6 with whatever they happened to have lying around. The result, in a long invoice, was carnage.

A Receipt, Set Properly

If the till had used a kinder typeface, the receipt would have read:

Sandwich £4.50 · Coffee £3.20 · Apple £0.80 · Service £2.24   Total £10.74

Tanya kept the bad receipt in a drawer, alongside a clipping from a 1972 train timetable in which the 17:14 to Carlisle had, due to a printer’s malfunction, become the 17:I4. Both, she felt, deserved to be remembered.
