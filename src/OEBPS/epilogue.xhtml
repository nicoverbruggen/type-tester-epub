Epilogue

The Sign in the Window

The sign had taken her three weeks to decide on.

She had kept a list. Fourteen typefaces in the first pass; seven after eliminating the ones that were too formal, too casual, too recently fashionable, or too likely to be confused with a dental practice. Three after eliminating the ones she had worked with so often that seeing them in her own window every morning would feel like speaking to someone she had already said everything to. One, finally: a face she had set with once on a commission in 2001, had not used since, and had been thinking about at odd moments ever since. She had the name cut in vinyl on a Wednesday afternoon, and had stood on the pavement opposite and looked at it for longer than was strictly necessary, until a man walking a dog had glanced at her with the mild concern of someone wondering whether she required assistance.

She had not required assistance. She had needed to be certain. She was.

Mr. Trevelyan had sent a note when she left. Characteristically brief. She had filed it, and thought about it occasionally, and found she was glad he had written it.

The studio had good light. She had chosen it partly for the light.

She had been working on a commission — a set of covers for a series whose previous designer had, in her professional opinion, been working without adequate supervision — when the envelope arrived. She recognised the handwriting. She set the covers aside, which they deserved.

Inside: a manuscript. On the cover page, in Yvette’s hand, a title she had not seen before.

She looked at it for a moment. Then she went to the file drawer and found the blank label she had put there in March on a train going south, and wrote the title on it in ink. She held it briefly before filing it.

Then she cleared a space on the desk, made tea, and began to read.
