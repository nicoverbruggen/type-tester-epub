Chapter Four

A Decimal Point Misplaced

It had been a Thursday. The receipt had been a disaster of the kind that could only have been produced by a machine actively working against Tanya’s interests. It claimed she had spent £1,074 on lunch, which would have been remarkable even by London standards and would have required, at minimum, a second table and some kind of structural support for the bread basket. The real total, arrived at after considerable forensic work and a conversation with the café owner that neither of them enjoyed, was £10.74. Somewhere between the till and the printer, whatever system had processed the transaction had silently dropped the decimal point, converting a perfectly reasonable lunch into a figure that would have given her accountant a small but sincere cardiac episode.

The receipt reminded her, as such incidents occasionally did, of a particular category of disaster: the kind that occurred not through any failure of human attention, but through a computer’s sincere and misguided attempt to read what a printer had produced. The two parties were not always well acquainted. A zero that had adopted the general silhouette of a capital O, a one that bore an uncomfortable resemblance to a lowercase l, a five that had been spending too much time with the letter S: none of these presented any difficulty to a careful human reader, who would apply context and goodwill in roughly equal measure. A machine, working quickly and without either, was a great deal less charitable. In a typeface that did not bother to keep its characters distinct, the results could be, and frequently were, creative.

A Receipt, Set Properly

Had the typeface kept its characters sufficiently distinct (had the zero carried a slash, or the one worn serifs, or the decimal point been given enough space to register as something other than a speck of ambient debris), the system might have stood a chance. The receipt, properly set, would have read:

Sandwich £4.50 · Coffee £3.20 · Apple £0.80 · Shortbread £2.24   Total £10.74

Tanya kept the bad receipt in a drawer, alongside a clipping from a 1972 train timetable in which the 17:14 to Carlisle had become, due to a printer’s malfunction, the 17:I4, departing from Platform ll at a time that could not be determined with confidence. No one, she imagined, had caught that train. She was not entirely certain the train had existed. Both documents she considered essential evidence. Both were filed, with some ceremony, under a hand-written label that read “The Defence Rests.”
