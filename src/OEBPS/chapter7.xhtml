Chapter Seven

A Conversation at the Window

It was the last week of October. Yvette had come down from Edinburgh for the launch of Waves on the Water, and had insisted, against Tanya’s protestations, on buying the wine. They sat by the window of the small flat above the typewriter factory, watching the rain fall on the car park.

“You’ve been quiet,” Yvette said.

“I’m always quiet.”

“You’re unusually quiet.”

Tanya turned her glass on its base. The wine, she noticed, was the same colour as the streetlamps reflected in it. “I’ve been thinking about leaving.”

“The factory?”

“The factory. The flat. The whole —” she gestured vaguely at the window, “— the whole arrangement.”

“And go where?”

“I don’t know. Somewhere with better light.”

Yvette laughed. “You can’t move countries because of the lighting, Tanya.”

“Why not? People move countries for less.”

“People move countries for love. For work. For tax reasons. They don’t move countries because the — what is it — the kerning is better in Antwerp.”

“The kerning is better in Antwerp,” Tanya said.

“I’m sure it is.”

“And the light.”

“And the light. Yes.”

They sat for a while. Outside, a man was struggling to fold an umbrella. He was losing.

“I read your book,” Tanya said, after a long pause. “On the train.”

“And?”

“And I didn’t lose my place. Not once.”

Yvette turned. “That’s — that’s the typeface you chose, isn’t it. The new one.”

“The new one. Yes.”

“Is that — is that good?”

Tanya considered the question. The man with the umbrella had given up and was carrying it, deflated, towards a waiting taxi. The wine in her glass had not moved.

“It’s the best thing I’ve done,” she said. “In thirty years.”

Yvette refilled both glasses. “Then you’re not going anywhere,” she said. “You’re going to stay here and set the next one.”

“Am I.”

“You are.”

And, in the event, she was.
