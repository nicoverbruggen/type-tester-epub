Chapter Five

Foreign Tongues

In the spring of 2003, Tanya had taken the night ferry to Hoek van Holland, ridden a bicycle through the polders to a café outside Brügge, and tried, in indifferent French, to order a croque-monsieur. The waiter had replied in better English than her own. The menu, set in a typeface designed by someone who clearly disapproved of accents, had read CROQUE-MONS EUR.

The diaeresis on her own name — the small dots that no British keyboard could quite produce — had been a lifelong source of administrative grief. Half the world spelt her name Tanya; the other half spelt it Tanja; her grandmother, who had been born in Salzburg, had spelt it Tan̈ja, with a combining diaeresis above the j that no font in living memory had ever rendered correctly.

The Accented Alphabet, Visited

A small grand tour of the diacritics that distinguish é from e, ñ from n, and å from a. A typeface that handles ascii cleanly but botches its accents is, in the end, a typeface for tourists.

The waiter, that long-ago day, had brought her the sandwich anyway. It had been excellent. The menu, she suspected, had been set on a machine older than she was.
