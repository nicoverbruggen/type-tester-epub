Chapter Six

Poems and Whitespace

Yvette’s manuscript, when Tanya finally read it through, turned out to be neither prose nor reference but verse — thirty-one short poems on water, weather, and waiting. Verse, she realised, was the cruelest test of any typeface. Prose can hide a multitude of tracking sins behind its long lines and confident punctuation. Verse, with its short lines, its centred stanzas, its hard returns and its long reflective silences, has nowhere to hide.

From Waves on the Water, by Yvette

i. The First Ferry

A small boat,

a wide river,

two gulls,

no wind.

 

The pilot lights a cigarette.

The passengers do not speak.

The gulls have nothing to say.

ii. Tide Table, Margate

Low at six.

High at noon.

Low again before the lamps come on.

The sea forgets nothing.

The sea remembers nothing.

The sea, I sometimes think,

does both at once.

iii. Nocturne for a Rented Room

A wardrobe.

A kettle.

A window that looks at another window.

In the other window, a lamp,

and behind the lamp, a man

who is also writing a poem

about a window.

iv. A Short Pangram for the Tired Eye

The quick brown fox

jumps over

the lazy dog.

 

It does not jump well.

It is not, today, in the mood.

The dog, for its part,

does not particularly mind.

Notes on Setting Verse

Tanya, who had been setting verse for the firm’s small-press division for over a decade, had three rules. First, never justify a poem; the white space between words is part of its breathing. Second, leave the indents alone; if the poet wanted a hanging indent, the poet would have asked for one. Third, if a line breaks across the page, the typesetter has chosen the wrong page size, not the wrong line break.

Most typefaces, set into verse, behave themselves. A few do not. Tanya could think, off the top of her head, of three faces in current commercial use whose lowercase y dropped so far below the baseline that it tangled with the ascenders of the next line, turning every two-line stanza into a small, embarrassed knot.
