Chapter Six

The Breath Between

Yvette’s manuscript, when Tanya finally read it all the way through on a rainy Sunday in February, turned out to be neither prose nor reference material but verse: thirty-one short poems on water, weather, and waiting, written in a hand that suggested someone who thought quickly and typed slowly and had given up reconciling the two. Verse, Tanya realised, was the cruelest test she could have set herself. Prose could conceal a multitude of tracking sins behind its long and confident lines and its busy punctuation. Verse had no such defences. With its short lines, its deliberate silences, and its carefully considered white space, it stood fully exposed to whatever the typesetter chose to do with it. There was nowhere to hide, and nothing between the poem and the reader but the page itself and the choices made on it.

From Waves on the Water, by Yvette

i. The First Ferry

A small boat,

a wide river,

two gulls,

no wind.

 

The pilot lights a cigarette.

The passengers do not speak.

The gulls have nothing to say.

ii. Tide Table, Margate

Low at six.

High at noon.

Low again before the lamps come on.

The sea forgets nothing.

The sea remembers nothing.

The sea, I sometimes think,

does both at once.

iii. Nocturne for a Rented Room

A wardrobe.

A kettle.

A window that looks at another window.

In the other window, a lamp,

and behind the lamp, a man

who is also writing a poem

about a window.

iv. A Short Pangram for the Tired Eye

The quick brown fox

jumps over

the lazy dog.

 

It does not jump well.

It is not, today, in the mood.

The dog, for its part,

does not particularly mind.

Notes on Setting Verse

Tanya, who had been setting verse for the firm’s small-press division for the better part of a decade, had arrived at three rules through a combination of accumulated experience and the specific misery of seeing things go wrong in print with no possibility of correction. First: never justify a poem. The white space to the right of each line was not emptiness — it was the poem catching its breath, and if you filled it with words you had changed the poem without consulting the poet, which was, in her view, a category of rudeness comparable to finishing someone’s sentences and getting them wrong. Second: leave the indentation alone. If the poet had wanted a hanging indent, the poet would have asked for one. They had not asked because they did not want one. Third: if a line breaks where it should not, the problem is the page size, not the line. Adjust accordingly, and do not blame the poem.

Most typefaces, when set into verse, had the good grace to behave themselves. A handful did not. Tanya could think, without effort, of three faces in current commercial use whose lowercase y descended so far below the baseline that it reached into the ascenders of the line below, tangling with every word that happened to begin with an h or a d or an l, turning a two-stanza meditation on the sea into something that looked, at its worst, like a ransom note composed by someone with very strong feelings about the ocean and very little time for punctuation.
